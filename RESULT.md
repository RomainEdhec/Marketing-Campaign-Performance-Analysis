Results and Business recommandations :

SUMMARY :

-High income customers generate the majority of revenue (71%) and show the highest engagement with marketing camapaigns (highest response rate, 24%), making them primary target for value-driven strategies

-Low income customers however, are more price-sensitive, with a higher proportion of purchases made using discountts and a premium product share of 29% vs 13% and 7% for medium and high incomer, suggesting more targeted or promotion-driven purchases

-Marital status has limited ipract on customer behavior, with relatively homogenous results across groups, indicating it is not a strong segmentation variable.

-A strong overlap exists between income and education levels (high income is linked with master/PhD, whereas low income is linked with Basic/2nd cycle), suggesting income is the primary driver of behavior.

DETAILED ANALYSIS : 

1. Revenue Contribution & Customer Value

High income customers clearly dominate in terms of total revenue contribution. Their significantly higher total spending per customer leads them to represent the largest share of overall turnover.

This confirms that value is highly concentrated within this segment, making it strategically critical for revenue maximization.

In contrast, low income customers contribute less in absolute terms but still represent an opportunity for growth through adapted strategies.

2. Marketing Performance

High income customers show:

-Higher response rates
-Higher campaign acceptance rates
-Higher overall marketing responsiveness

This indicates that high-value customers are also highly engaged, which is not always expected and represents a strong opportunity.

It suggests that marketing campaigns are already well aligned with this segment, and further optimization could yield strong returns.

3. Purchase Behavior & Price Sensitivity

Clear behavioral differences emerge between segments:

High income customers :

-Lower use of discounts (9%)

-Lower premium ratio (7%)

-Higher overall spending (71%)

Indicates diversified and less price-sensitive consumption

Low income customers :

-Higher use of discounts (38%)

-Higher premium ratio (29%)

-Lower overall spending (5%)

Suggests more selective purchasing behavior, potentially driven by promotions or specific needs

This contrast highlights two distinct consumption logics:

volume and diversification vs targeted and price-sensitive purchasing

4. Role of Marital Status

Across all KPIs (spending, response rates, purchase behavior), marital status does not show significant variation.

This suggests that marital status is not a key driver of customer behavior in this dataset and should not be prioritized for segmentation.

5. Income vs Education

A clear pattern appears between income and education levels:

High income customers are associated with higher education levels (Master, PhD)
Low income customers are more represented in lower education levels

This indicates that education acts as a proxy for income, and that income remains the most relevant segmentation variable.

Business Recommandations : 

1. Prioritize High Income Segments (value strategy)

Focus campaigns on high income customers to maximize ROI

Promote premium positioning rather than discounts

Develop loyalty for this high-value segment

2. Use promotions strategically for low income segments

Leverage discounts to drive engagements and conversions

Design targeted promotional campaigns 

Focus on increasing purchase frequency rather than basket size

3. Avoid marital status for targeting

Limited impact on behavior

Not relevant for segmentation or campaign personalization

4. Structure marketing strategy around two core segments : 

Premium segment : high income, high value, low price sensitivity

Price-sensitive segment : low income, promotion-driven behavior
